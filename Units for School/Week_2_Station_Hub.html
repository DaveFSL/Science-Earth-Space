It's the Goldilocks balance from the Orbit Lab: gravity pulls inwards, motion carries forward.

3. In the Orbit Lab, what happened the instant gravity was switched off?

A straight line — objects keep moving straight unless a force bends their path. That's why the orbit needs gravity.

Station A · 20 min

Rotation or Revolution? (teacher-led)

👩‍🏫 Station leader card — read this FIRST if you are running this station (teacher, aide or student leader) — or if your group is working without a leader

Run sheet (20 minutes):

  1. Demonstrate first (5 min). Hold the globe. Say: "Watch carefully — I'm going to move it two different ways." First spin it on its axis (that's rotation — moving around itself). Then carry it in a circle around the torch (that's revolution — travelling around something else). Repeat both while the group says the words with you.
  2. Anchor the definitions (2 min). Rotation = spinning on your own axis, like a top. Revolution = travelling around another object, like a lap of an oval. The test question: "Is it moving around itself, or around something else?"
  3. Animations (8 min). Work through the six cards together. Before anyone clicks, ask one person to justify their choice out loud using the test question.
  4. Big questions (5 min). Everyone writes their own answers — discuss first, write second.

Targeted questions to ask (students: use these to check yourself later):

  • "Is it moving around itself or around something else?"
  • "Earth does both at once. How long does one rotation take? One revolution?"
  • "Which one explains our shadow data from last week — rotation or revolution? How do you know?"
  • "Why do we say the Sun rises if the Sun isn't actually moving across the sky?"
  • "If Earth rotated twice as fast, how long would a day be?"
  • Stretch: "The Moon rotates exactly once every time it revolves once around Earth. What would that mean for the side of the Moon we see?"

Listen for these misconceptions: "the Sun goes around the Earth" (our shadow data shows steady, predictable sweeping — caused by our turning); "rotation and revolution are the same thing" (return to the test question); "Earth's spin takes a year" (24 hours for a spin; 365¼ days for the lap).

Station B · 20 min

Build the working day/night model

What to do at this station

In your trio, follow the steps below to build a model that shows why Brisbane has daytime while London is asleep. Then rehearse your 30-second voice note — you'll record it at the plenary. Tick each step's check before moving on.

1

Gather your kit: one torch (or lamp), one globe (or ball on a skewer), two small sticker dots, and a stack of 2–3 books.

✅ Check: you have all four things before you start.

2

Set up your "Sun": rest the torch on the books about 30 cm from the globe, so the beam points at the middle of the globe — not up at the top or down at the base.

✅ Check: torch is level with the globe's equator and doesn't need holding.

3

Mark your cities: put one sticker on Brisbane (east coast of Australia) and one on London (United Kingdom). On a real globe, find them first — ask the group if unsure. They're nearly on opposite sides of the world.

✅ Check: both stickers on, both names said out loud.

4

Lights off, torch on. Look carefully: exactly half the globe is lit. The lit half is having daytime; the dark half is having night — right now, at the same moment.

✅ Check: each person points to the day side and the night side.

5

Make the Earth turn: rotate the globe slowly from west to east (anticlockwise when you look down on the North Pole). Watch your Brisbane sticker travel from darkness into light — that exact edge moment is sunrise. Keep turning: midday is when Brisbane faces the torch square-on; sunset is leaving the light; midnight is facing directly away.

✅ Check: your trio can show sunrise, midday, sunset and midnight in Brisbane on request.

6

The detective question: position the globe so your Brisbane sticker is in full light. Now find London. Light or dark? That's why a 7 pm video call to London reaches someone at breakfast — same planet, opposite sides of the turn.

✅ Check: 📷 photograph your model showing Brisbane lit and London dark → OneNote → Science → Week 2.
