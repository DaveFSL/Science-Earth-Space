Misconception clinic \u2014 every foreseeable error, with the fix

Open the one your data flags red. Each shows the wrong idea, the correct idea, and a quick reteach you can do on the spot. Great for revision too \u2014 students can reopen this any time.

🌞 "The Sun moves around the Earth"

The wrong idea: the Sun travels across the sky / around the Earth each day.

The correct idea: the Sun stays put; Earth rotates west-to-east, so the Sun only appears to move.

Reteach in 60 seconds: spin a student slowly on a chair facing a "Sun" (a lamp or a poster). As they turn, the lamp seems to move across their view \u2014 but everyone else can see it's the student turning. "You are the Earth. The Sun didn't move \u2014 you did."

  • Ask: "What evidence do we have that we're the ones turning?" (the steady, predictable shadow sweep from Week 1).
  • Link: this is exactly the geocentric mistake we'll put on trial in Week 6.
🔥 "Seasons happen because Earth gets closer to the Sun"

The wrong idea: summer is when Earth is nearer the Sun; winter is when it's further.

The correct idea: seasons come from the 23.5° tilt. The hemisphere leaning towards the Sun has summer. Distance barely matters.

The clincher: Earth is actually closest to the Sun in early January \u2014 the middle of OUR summer but the middle of the Northern Hemisphere's winter. If distance caused seasons, the whole planet would be hot at once. It isn't.

  • Reopen the Season Machine and press "tilt OFF": daylight locks at 12 hours, seasons vanish. Tilt is the cause.
  • Ask: "If it's summer here and winter in the UK at the same moment, can distance be the reason?"
🧭 "Earth's tilt changes through the year"

The wrong idea: Earth straightens up in summer and tips over more in winter; or the axis swings to point at the Sun.

The correct idea: the tilt is fixed at 23.5° and the axis always points the same way in space. It's Earth's position on its orbit that changes which hemisphere leans sunward.

Reteach: walk a tilted globe in a big circle around a "Sun", keeping the axis pointing at the same wall the whole time. Stop at four points: same tilt, different hemisphere lit. "The lean never changed \u2014 only where Earth is."

🔁 "Rotate and revolve mean the same thing"

The wrong idea: the two words are interchangeable; or rotation takes a year / revolution takes a day.

The correct idea: Rotate = spin on your own axis (Earth: ~24 hours = a day). Revolve = travel around something else (Earth around the Sun: ~365¼ days = a year).

The golden question: "Is it moving around itself, or around something else?" Use the spinning chair (rotate) vs walking a lap of the room (revolve).

  • Quick check: "Which one gives us day and night? Which gives us a year?"
🪐 "Bigger planets are further from the Sun" / planet order muddles

The wrong idea: size decides distance; or the inner/outer order is jumbled.

The correct idea: order out from the Sun is Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars (small, rocky), then Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune (giants). Size and distance aren't linked \u2014 tiny Mercury is closest; giant Jupiter is fifth.

Reteach: re-run the pocket solar system halving trick \u2014 the inner four bunch near the Sun. Pair it with a class mnemonic.

🛰 "What keeps Earth in orbit?" (gravity-only or motion-only)

The wrong idea: gravity alone holds Earth (so why doesn't it fall in?), or there's no gravity in space so motion alone keeps it going.

The correct idea: it's a balance \u2014 the Sun's gravity pulls inward while Earth's forward motion would fly off in a straight line. Together they bend the path into an orbit.

Reteach: reopen the Orbit Lab. Too slow \u2192 crash (gravity wins). Too fast \u2192 escape (motion wins). Snap gravity off \u2192 straight line. The orbit needs both.

📏 "A model is wrong if it isn't perfectly accurate"

The wrong idea: a model that gets something wrong (e.g. planet sizes) is a bad model.

The correct idea: every model trades off accuracy to make something clearer. A good scientist knows what their model shows well and badly.

Reteach: the pocket solar system shows distance brilliantly but size and motion poorly \u2014 and that's fine, because we used it to study distance. Knowing the limits IS the skill (and the assessment rewards it).

🌗 "The Moon makes its own light" / Moon & Sun confusion (pre-empt for Arc 2)

The wrong idea: the Moon glows by itself; or the Moon and Sun are similar objects.

The correct idea: the Moon reflects sunlight \u2014 it's a rocky world, not a light source. The Sun is a star. (This surfaces in the model-examples and Arc 2.)

Reteach: shine a torch on a ball in a dark room \u2014 the ball "glows" only because it's lit. No torch, no glow.
