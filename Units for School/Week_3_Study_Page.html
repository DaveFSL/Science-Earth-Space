Mission Briefing — 5 questions, marks itself

1. How long does Earth take to rotate once on its axis?

One spin = one day = about 24 hours. The year-long one is the revolution around the Sun.

2. Why is it daytime here while it's night-time in the UK?

Your Station B model showed it: the lit half has day, the dark half has night, and the rotation swaps them every 24 hours.

3. At about what time of day is a stick's shadow shortest?

Your own shadow data showed it: the higher the Sun, the shorter the shadow — and the Sun is highest around the middle of the day.

4. What keeps Earth travelling in its orbit around the Sun?

The Orbit Lab balance: gravity pulls inwards, motion carries forward. Too slow = crash; too fast = escape.

5. Which direction does Earth rotate?

Earth turns west to east, so the Sun seems to rise in the east and set in the west — even though the Sun isn't the one moving.

The Shadow Files — unsealing the case 🔓

🔒

Last week you recorded a hunch: why does the Monday 1 pm shadow shrink, week after week, with the same stick at the same time?

Open your hunch in OneNote and read it out. Then…

CASE SOLVED: the Sun is climbing.

Every week, the 1 pm Sun sits a little higher in our sky — and a higher Sun makes a shorter shadow (your own data from Week 1 proved that across a single day; this is the same rule across a whole term).

But why is the Sun climbing? Because Earth is tilted, and as it revolves around the Sun, our half of the planet — the Southern Hemisphere — is slowly leaning further towards the Sun. Winter is ending. By the September equinox, day and night are equal, and the Sun stands much higher at 1 pm than it did in July.

So your Monday photo strip isn't just a stack of pictures. It's evidence that Earth is tilted and on the move — collected by you, with a stick and some chalk. That's real science.

Big ideas

Four ideas that explain every season on Earth

Your teacher will walk through these in class. Come back to them any time — especially before the assessment. Each card is one idea, one picture, one sentence to remember.

1Earth leans

Earth doesn't spin standing straight up. Its axis — the invisible line it spins around — leans over at 23.5°, like a spinning top that's tipped but never falls.

Remember: Earth is permanently tilted at 23.5°.

2The lean never changes direction

As Earth travels its year-long lap of the Sun, the axis keeps pointing at the same spot in space the whole way round — it does not wobble towards the Sun and back.

Remember: the tilt stays fixed — it's Earth's position on the orbit that changes.

3Lean towards = summer. Lean away = winter.

The hemisphere leaning towards the Sun gets sunlight that's more direct and days that are longer — that's its summer. Six months later, the same hemisphere leans away: weaker, slanted light and shorter days — winter. The hemispheres always have opposite seasons.

Remember: towards = summer, away = winter — at the very same moment, on the very same planet.

4BUSTED: it's the tilt, not the distance ❌

Earth is actually closest to the Sun in January — the middle of our summer, but the middle of winter for the Northern Hemisphere. If distance caused the seasons, the whole planet would have summer at once. It doesn't — because it's the tilt.

Detective's clincher: January = closest AND northern winter. Distance is innocent. The tilt did it.
