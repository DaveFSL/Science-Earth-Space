Mission Briefing — 5 questions, marks itself

1. Why did the model of the solar system change over hundreds of years? (Week 6)

Models change with new evidence, and discoveries build on earlier ones — like Galileo using Copernicus's idea.

2. What causes the seasons?

The 23.5° tilt — the hemisphere leaning towards the Sun has summer. Seasons matter today: sky patterns track them!

3. What is a good scientific method for predicting nature?

Observe → pattern → predict. That's exactly what your shadow investigation did — and what the first astronomers did for millennia.

4. Your shadow photos got shorter each week because…

Same stick, higher Sun = shorter shadow. Tracking the sky to mark the seasons is the heart of today's lesson.

5. The Milky Way is…

The Milky Way is our galaxy. The dark patches within it matter a lot in today's lesson!

Explicit teaching

The science of the first astronomers

Your teacher will walk through these in class. The big idea: First Nations Peoples used the exact same scientific thinking you've been using — careful observation, finding patterns, and predicting — to read the sky as a living calendar. Come back to these cards for revision and for Part B of your assessment.

1The world's first scientists

Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Peoples have observed and recorded the sky for more than 65,000 years — far longer than any other culture on Earth, and tens of thousands of years before the Greek astronomers we met last week.

Remember: the first astronomers were the First Peoples of Australia.

2The sky as a calendar

By watching which stars appear at which times of year, First Nations Peoples knew when to find particular foods, when animals would breed, and when seasons would change — a precise, reliable calendar written in the stars.

Remember: repeated sky observation → patterns → accurate predictions about the land.

3Gugurmin — the Emu in the Sky

Here's the clever part. The Gugurmin (Emu in the Sky) isn't made of stars — it's made of the dark dust clouds between them, stretching across the Milky Way. Its position through the year tells people about emu breeding and egg-collecting season.

Remember: the Emu is read from the darkness between the stars — a unique way of seeing the sky.

4Tagai — the Torres Strait navigator

For the peoples of the Torres Strait, Tagai is a great figure standing in a canoe, formed from many stars. Where Tagai sits in the sky signals the seasons — when to plant gardens, and when turtles and other foods are ready.

Remember: Tagai is a star calendar guiding planting, fishing and harvest in the Torres Strait.

Interactive

Find the Emu in the Sky 🦤

Most people look for pictures made of stars. The Gugurmin is different — it's made of the dark spaces. Press the button to reveal the Emu hidden in the Milky Way, then read how its changing position works as a calendar.

When the Emu first appears stretched across the sky in autumn, it looks like it's running — a sign that emus are laying eggs. Later in the year its position changes, signalling when egg-collecting should stop so emus can raise their young. The sky tells people not just when to gather food, but when to stop, so there's always more next year — sustainable science, thousands of years before the word existed.
