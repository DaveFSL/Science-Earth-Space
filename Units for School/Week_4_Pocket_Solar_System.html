Build a Pocket Solar System

Squeeze the whole solar system onto a strip of paper — and discover what a model can and can't show.

← All lessons

Today you're a model-maker. Real scientists use models to make huge things small enough to study. You'll fold the eight planets onto a one-metre strip using a clever halving trick — then judge how good your model really is, just like a scientist would.

Working through the steps: read each one, do it, then press Next step ▶. You can always go ◀ Back to check.

1

Gather your kit

Before you start, collect everything you need so you don't have to stop halfway.

Do this: gather a 1-metre strip of paper (or tape sheets together end-to-end), a ruler, a pencil, and coloured pencils. Lay the strip flat and long-ways in front of you.

Tip: if your strip is a little shorter or longer than a metre, that's fine — the halving trick still works.

2

Mark the two ends

The two ends of your strip are the two ends of the solar system.

Do this: at the far left end, draw and label the Sun. At the far right end, draw a small dot and label it Neptune — the most distant planet.

Everything else fits between these two points. Notice how much empty space there'll be — that's a clue for later!

3

The halving trick — find each planet by folding

Here's the clever bit. The planets aren't spread out evenly — they bunch up closer to the Sun. The halving trick handles that automatically.

Do this, in order:
  1. Fold the strip so the Sun meets Neptune. The crease in the middle is Uranus. Open it out and label it.
  2. Now fold so the Sun meets Uranus. That new crease is Saturn. Label it.
  3. Fold Sun to Saturn → the crease is Jupiter. Label it.

See the pattern? Each fold halves the leftover gap — and each planet sits closer to the Sun than the last.

4

Squeeze in the inner planets

The four inner planets — Mercury, Venus, Earth and Mars — are all crammed into the small gap between the Sun and Jupiter.

Do this: in the space between the Sun and Jupiter, mark the four inner planets close together near the Sun, in this order out from the Sun:
Mercury Venus Earth Mars

They're so close together they'll almost touch — that's exactly right. The inner solar system really is squashed up near the Sun.

5

Colour and finish your model

Make it a model you'd be proud to show.

Do this: colour each planet, double-check all eight are labelled in the right order, and add a title: "My Pocket Solar System".
Out from the SunPlanets in order
Inner (rocky)Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars
Outer (giants)Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune

6

Think like a scientist: evaluate your model

No model is perfect — and a good scientist always knows the limits of their model. Your pocket solar system is brilliant at showing one thing, but it gets two things very wrong on purpose. Let's judge it.

Scientists noticed these too:
  • Shows well: the real distances — especially how the planets bunch near the Sun and how huge the gaps are out past Jupiter.
  • Gets wrong: the planet sizes (on a true scale, if the strip were a metre, even Jupiter would be a tiny speck and Earth almost invisible); it can't show planets moving or orbiting; and the planets aren't really lined up in a row — they're scattered around their orbits.
  • Could improve: a 3D model could show orbits and movement; a digital model could show size and distance by letting you zoom.
📸

Evidence check: screenshot your three evaluation answers → OneNote → Science → Week 4. Knowing what a model shows well and badly is exactly the thinking your assessment rewards!

🏁

Case closed — nice work, model-maker

You've built a model, judged its strengths and weaknesses, and thought like a real scientist. That last skill — knowing what a model shows well and badly — is gold for your assessment task later in the term.

Challenge for fast finishers: if your 1-metre strip is the real distance to Neptune (about 4.5 billion km), work out what just 1 cm of your strip represents in real space.
