Key idea

Gravity + motion = orbit

What keeps the planets in orbit? Think of a ball on a string 🎾

Swing a ball on a string around in a circle. Two things make it orbit your hand: the ball's motion (you got it moving!) and the string pulling it inwards. Let go of the string and the ball flies off in a straight line. Don't get it moving and it just hangs down.

Space works the same way. A planet's motion tries to carry it in a straight line, while the Sun's gravity pulls it inwards — and the balance between the two bends its path into an orbit. There is no string in space. Gravity is the invisible string.

The Shadow Files — our term-long evidence

The plan

Our question: How does a stick's shadow change in length and direction during a school day?

Keeping it fair — every measurement uses the same stick, the same spot, and the same method: measure from the base of the stick to the tip of the shadow, to the nearest centimetre, and record the direction with the compass.

Shadow data table

Record outside on your paper recording sheet, then type your results here (or straight into this table if you take a laptop out). Your trio's roster covers the times your class isn't outside.

📅 Time-lapse mission (all term): one trio photographs the same stick every Monday at exactly 1 pm. By Week 8 you'll have a photo series proving the shadows are slowly changing week by week — evidence of something big. What could it be? Photos → the class Shadow Files page on OneNote.

Word bank

Speak like a scientist

solar system
the Sun and everything that orbits it — eight planets, their moons, asteroids and comets
gravity
an invisible force that pulls objects with mass towards each other; the Sun's enormous mass gives it an enormous pull
orbit
the curved path an object follows around a larger body — made by the balance of motion and gravity
evidence
information from observation or measurement that supports (or challenges) a claim — a detective's favourite word
