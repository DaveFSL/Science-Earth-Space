Mission Briefing — 5 questions, marks itself

1. What really causes the seasons?

It's the tilt, not distance — Earth is actually closest to the Sun in January (our summer, the north's winter).

2. Why does the Sun appear to move across our sky each day?

The Sun only appears to move. We are turning — west to east — which is the whole point of today's mystery.

3. What keeps Earth in orbit around the Sun?

The Orbit Lab balance: gravity pulls in, motion carries forward. Newton explained this — and he's on trial today!

4. What is a scientific model?

A model is a thinking tool — and a good one changes when better evidence comes along. That's today's big idea.

5. A detective's claim is only as good as its…

Claims need evidence — our motto since Week 1, and the rule the whole Science Court runs on.

How the model changed — tap each clue

Your teacher will walk through these in class. Each card is one detective in history. Tap a card to see the evidence they found and — importantly — whose work they built on. Come back to these before your assessment: this is Part B gold.

~150 AD

Ptolemy — Earth at the centre

Ancient Greek astronomer · Egypt

👆 Tap for the evidence

Ptolemy put Earth at the centre, with the Sun, Moon and planets circling it. It actually matched what people saw — the Sun really does appear to cross the sky — and it predicted planet positions well enough to last over 1,400 years.

Why it made sense: it agreed with everyday observation — just like your Week 2 exit ticket said the Sun "rises". A reasonable model for its evidence.
1543

Copernicus — Sun at the centre

Nicolaus Copernicus · Poland

👆 Tap for the evidence

Copernicus proposed a daring idea: put the Sun at the centre and let Earth be just another planet orbiting it. It made the maths of planet motion much simpler.

Built on: Ptolemy's careful records — he used the same observations but rearranged them into a simpler model. He didn't start from scratch.
1610

Galileo — the telescope proof

Galileo Galilei · Italy

👆 Tap for the evidence

Galileo pointed a new invention — the telescope — at the sky and saw four moons orbiting Jupiter. That proved not everything circles the Earth. He also saw the phases of Venus, which only work if Venus orbits the Sun.

Built on: Copernicus's Sun-centred idea (he found the evidence for it) AND the telescope makers of his time, whose lenses he improved.
1687

Newton — why they orbit at all

Isaac Newton · England

👆 Tap for the evidence

Newton explained the why: the same gravity that makes an apple fall keeps the planets curving around the Sun. The exact balance you discovered in the Orbit Lab.

Built on: Galileo's work on how things move, and Kepler's discovery that orbits are ellipses. Newton said he "stood on the shoulders of giants" — the perfect description of how science works.

The Science Court — the trial of the geocentric model

The case before the court

For 1,400 years, everyone "knew" the Earth sat still at the centre of everything. Today, that idea is on trial. Your class will argue it out — and the jury must decide using evidence, not opinion.

Your teacher will give you a role. Find yours below, read your job, and prepare your strongest evidence.

🏛 Team Ptolemy (defence)

You defend the geocentric model. Your strongest points:

  • It matches what everyone sees — the Sun really does appear to cross the sky.
  • We don't feel the Earth moving, so why believe it does?
  • It predicted the planets well for over 1,400 years.

🔭 Team Galileo (prosecution)

You argue the model is wrong. Your evidence:

  • Jupiter has moons orbiting it — so not everything orbits Earth.
  • Venus has phases that only work if it orbits the Sun.
  • Our own shadow data is explained better by a spinning Earth.

⚖ The Jury

You decide — but only on evidence, not on who spoke loudest. For each side, write down the strongest piece of evidence you heard, then give your verdict and your reason.

👩‍⚖️ The Judge

You keep order and make sure every claim is backed by evidence. After each point, ask the magic question: "What's your evidence for that?"
